Alan Turing

Introduction:

Alan Turing was a computer scientist at Bletchley Park throughout World War Two. He was born on 23rd June 1912, in London, England. During World War Two, he worked at Hut 8 of Bletchley Park where he helped to improve a pre-war Polish machine used to crack ciphers, known as a bombe, and was influential in the process of cracking the Enigma, for which he was awarded an OBE.

Early Life & Schooling

Alan Turing was born in London, where he spent most of his early life and holidays after enrolling in a boarding school at the age of 13. He presented a particular interest and skill in Mathematics and science at a young age, much to the dislike of many teachers at his boarding school, who focused more on classical studies than subjects like science and Mathematics.

Between 1931 and 1934, Turing studied Mathematics as an undergraduate at Cambridge's King's College. He achieved a first-class honours in Mathematics, and went on to get a PhD in mathematics at Princeton University.

Work at Bletchley Park

At Bletchley Park, Turing primarily worked on cryptanalysis of the Enigma and cracking of the Enigma. He also produced the specification for the Bombe - a machine that searched for possible settings for the Enigma machine. This machine proved to be crucial in cracking the cipher, which led to the decryption on many messages detailing planned attacks from the Axis Powers, and the prevention of these attacks.

Life Post WW2

Post World War Two, Alan Turing worked on many projects, including proposing the Turing Test and working on the first videogame, Turochamp.

Unfortunately, in March 1952 Turing was convicted of gross indecency due to being gay (homosexual acts being illegal in the UK at the time). He was made to undergo hormonal changes, such as oestrogen injections, and he later committed suicide on 8th June 1954, through a fatal dosage of cyanide.

On 24th December 2013, Turing was pardoned.
